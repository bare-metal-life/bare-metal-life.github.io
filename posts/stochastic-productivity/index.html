Stochastic Productivity and ADHD

July 24, 2024

My last article was about exponential productivity. This one is about another kind of productivity that goes along with a typical person with ADHD.

If you do a Google search about freelancing with ADHD, there are sorts of tips about time management, pomodoro, reducing distractions etc.

The problem with these is that the ADHD is treated as a monster that needs to be tamed. It’s much better to go with it and use it as a strength … which is why I have coined the term Stochastic Productivity.

The problem with all these ADHD freelancer tips is that they try to force you into creating a structure for your work … but the ADHD brain doesn’t work like that. I can be having my dinner when I suddenly realise the solution to something I’m working on. On the other hand, I can be sitting on my sofa during a period of time that I have theoretically assigned to “WORK” but my brain refuses to play ball.

Should I refuse to use the insight I had during dinner, because it is outside the assigned time for “WORK”? No, of course not! Otherwise that spurt of insight, energy and enthusiasm will be lost.

I have a lot of projects on the go at once. Learning Russian, improving my Thai, making kombucha etc, in addition, of course, to work projects.

My mind cycles between them … notwithstanding the issues I described in Exponential Productivity.

If I am staring at a piece of code in a time period assigned to “WORK” but my brain is energised by an idea from some other project, should I dampen that enthusiasm and try to divert my brain back to “WORK” … even though it’s going to be pulling me back to the other project it has this new bright idea about? No, that would be stupid!

But on the other hand, my client is in theory paying for my time.

I call this Stochastic Productivity: it happens at random, but when it happens it is deep and fast. This kind of productivity happens mostly during the slow period of the exponential productivity curve. I may stare at a block of code trying to figure it out … but my brain waits until I am driving my mum back to her house to reveal it to me in all its full glory.

The slow part of the exponential productivity curve is not a smooth progression. It proceeds in random jumps. Stochastic productivity.

There is another kind of stochastic productivity: it is related to problem solving for systems.

A famous story tells of a Ford factory that suddenly shuts down because of some mysterious problem that nobody can figure out. So the CEO calls Bob, a former employee. Bob turns up, thinks for a moment, dives into an electrical cabinet – one of many hundreds – and presses a small button in the corner of a crowded console. The factory leaps back into life. It took him all of 4 minutes.

He sends in a bill for a million dollars:

  • $4 for pressing the button
  • $999,996 for knowing which button to press.

A client can notify me of a certain problem … and I can fix it in minutes … but should I bill them just for the minutes I spent fixing it? In order for my working day to be worthwhile, I would have to be sent hundreds of problems every day.

If I were to only be paid for these random spikes of stochastic productivity, I would not be able to pay my bills! But if I bill for a day’s work in which I have some of these spikes … in between which I am doing nothing relevant to the actual work project … how can I justify it?

A person with linear productivity can obviously bill for a fair day’s work … I suppose I am billing for an acceptable number of stochastic productivity spikes within that day, regardless of what else I do?

I’ve never had complaints about my productivity from any client over my 30 year career as a freelance software developer, so I guess they are happy …
