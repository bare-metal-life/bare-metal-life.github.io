Anna Karenina

May 2, 2023

The book on my mother’s bedside table

I first encountered Anna when I was 17. I saw the book on my mother’s bedside table. The title immediately attracted me. Anna Karenina. The name sounded beautiful, Russian and mysterious. I started reading:

Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way.

If I’m not hooked by the first line of a book, it’s usually a sign I will struggle with the rest of the book. I was hooked by this line.

Railriders travel the nation

When I was 18, I wanted to choose a university to study astrophysics. I wanted to choose one with an impressive observatory and telescope and with some astronomical fame behind it. The UCAS system allowed me to rank five in descending order of desirability. I chose:

  • St Andrews
  • Edinburgh
  • Manchester
  • Birmingham
  • Bristol

British Rail were offering one-pound tickets for students going to open days and interviews at their chosen universities. This was a great opportunity for me to indulge my hobby of long distance rail travel.

St Andrews

St Andrews is the oldest university in the UK, but I experienced it as the coldest. I stayed overnight in bleak student accommodation in a bleak field over two bleak days. The town is beautiful but to me it was bleak too. I was disappointed. There’s something about the promise of an historical place … said promise rarely delivers. Even though the stones of buildings may be old, life is still modern. You don’t get to participate in Mediaeval life just by being in a town of Mediaeval origin, just as tuning the dial on a vintage radio won’t let you receive transmissions from when the radio was young.

On the way back to Sussex, I had a couple of hours to wait at Edinburgh. When I came out of Waverley Station I fell in love with this city. It is still the place I go to in my dreams most nights.

Edinburgh

There is simply too much to say about Edinburgh right now so I will get back to Anna Karenina.

The Penguin Paperback

Edinburgh had a huge Waterstones on Princes Street where I would spend my Saturday mornings browsing and dipping into this book and that on their comfy sofas. This was the golden age of the bookshop, before Amazon destroyed it. One Saturday morning, I saw her … Anna Karenina … in the reduced section. 50% off because she was damaged goods. Somebody had taken her off a shelf, sat down on a sofa with her, and stained her pages with coffee. I took her home.

The book finished with her death under the wheels of the train. She realised too late that she had made a fatefully wrong decision. But the book shouldn’t finish there! I read it again. Again, it finished with her being crushed by unstoppable steel.

I read it again. Same result.

I wanted a different outcome. I wanted her to step back in time.

I also wanted to know about Konstantin Levin and his Kitty. Anna’s final action seemed to suspend his storyline too. It was most unsatisfactory.

I read my spoiled copy many times since then, always wanting a different ending, always wondering about Konstantin and Kitty.

The Unabridged Translation

The Penguin Paperback was a fraud! It was a pathetically abridged version for Western readers wanting a tragic love story, who would be impatient with the spiritual journey of a patiently searching man. The best translation is by Rosamund Bartlett. The full edition does not end with the death of Anna, but with the spiritual resurrection of our Konstantin. This is of interest to neither seekers of romantic tragedy nor Liberal Western intellectuals. The abridged versions soften the impact of Tolstoy’s disdain of the latter type.

Anna Karenina has two goals: to give readers a gripping story and to show us a few general observations about society through the lens of a small group of characters. The character that Tolstoy uses most to achieve this second goal is Constantine (or Konstantin) Levin. … if there’s one thing Tolstoy admires in a guy, it’s a drive to understand and resolve intellectual and existential concerns.

But not just to resolve them … it’s a drive to understand the bare metal nature of life. To uncover the True Nature of the Things That Are and live accordingly.

  • The nature of family and marriage, first and foremost
  • The nature of the Russian Countryside in general
  • The nature of the Russian Peasant, now emancipated, in particular.

Levin doesn’t just want intellectual satisfaction. He wants to understand in order to live, or in terms of his faith, to understand in order to believe.

No other novel satisfies me … no other novel has a character with whom I can identify so strongly.
