Exponential Productivity

I am a freelancer, digital nomad, and sometimes an entrepreneur. As such, I am often required to account for my time to a client using the dreaded Timesheet. I always have a problem with this because:

I DO NOT KNOW HOW TO MEASURE THE TIME I SPEND ON A PROJECT!

And suddenly, recently, I understood why … because of a passing comment by my Russian teacher in one of her episodes about her life as a teacher, student and as a Russian outside Russia.

She said that she is fascinated by the process of learning a new skill through small, incremental, repeated steps.

I spent some time thinking about this, because it felt that it was different from my approach of consuming firehoses of information.

The lightbulb moment came when I combined her idea with the concept of saving money under a regime of compound interest. Small savings repeated over time along with interest payments on previously saved money: a sure way to build wealth. Of course, I realised, this is the way it is with language learning.

I was labouring under an unacknowledged assumption that language learning involves linear productivity (productivity being the amount of value created in a certain amount of time). But NO! It follows the law of compound interest … in other words, it is exponential.

Fortunately I have been able to track this (albeit imperfectly) with the main app I use for language learning: LingQ. It has a very useful statistical feature. At first, progress is slow, with small increments. Then as you carry on adding content with new vocabulary, you hit a very difficult and frustrating phase, often called the “Intermediate Plateau”. It seems like you will never make progress. Then at some point the compounding effect seems to kick in – I feel like I am at the start of this phase with Russian, and it’s very exciting! I feel that that painful, incremental steps are giving way to understanding more and more … although active spoken vocabulary lags well behind passive read vocabulary.

A vitally important factor is the speed at which the brain is able to assimilate new material to the point of familiarity.

Another important factor is that language acquisition is not primarily about conscious analysis of language structures. Familiarity comes first; the ability to analyse comes second.

But where else have I experienced this but not unacknowledged it? In my programming work, of course! This brings me back to the question of how to bill for time when productivity is not linear.

Most projects start with a phase of discovery. I look at what needs to be done – and often, the whole task is completely new to me. Of course, I may know the programming language and tech in use, but every project has a different set of data structures and business rules. I am starting from zero. It is exactly like learning a new language.

In the early stages I will look at some code but really have no idea how it all fits together.

The two factors above come into play here: I need to give my brain time to become familiar, but this only happens incrementally at the start. I cannot begin to analyse the structure before I have allowed my brain its due time. I cannot force or will this to go any faster.

Unfamiliarity forces the brain to use a lot of energy and it quickly settles back into some pattern that it is used to. This feels like procrastination. I used to berate myself, telling myself that I can’t bill the customer for these hours, because I’m just looking at the code for short bursts, then falling back to yet another YouTube video or playlist or nostalgic daydream. I would tell myself that I can’t possibly pass the cost of my ADHD on to my client!

But it’s not ADHD!

It is the first part of a productivity curve that will turn upwards, following the law of compound interest. As I get familiar with the codebase, there will come a point when my brain will start joining up the dots, subconsciously, without conscious effort, perhaps while I sleep, perhaps while I have those songs on loop, perhaps when I’m in the shower or shopping. (How to bill for this?)

Then, suddenly, I know what I am doing. There is an up-turn in productivity. I start to realise what I need to do to nail the first issue, then the next, then I get into my flow, and suddenly I am in that zone where I ignore my family for two days straight and come out with the result the client was wanting.

But still, I don’t know how to bill for exponential productivity because billing systems are based on linear productivity.

I also understand now why I experience firehose learning: this only happens if it’s something I already have a deep knowledge of. It’s like the compound interest effect has already taken effect and I am just adding to it.
